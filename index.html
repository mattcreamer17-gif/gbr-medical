The view from booth 323

You were at booth 323 in 2025. You are here again in 2026, and the month before this you were at CES under Longevity and Beauty Tech, and the month after this you are at the White and Private Label Expo in London. Seven product lines across four buyer worlds: clinical, wellness, longevity, private-label. Simplified Medical Technology is the tagline, but the operating reality is anything but simple. The Tuesday after each show is the expensive part. Leads arrive in different formats, buyers ask for different versions of the same evidence packet, and the follow-up either happens in the first ten days or it does not happen at all.

The Eduba frame

Most of what slows a seven-SKU medical device company after a trade show is not an AI problem. We would guess 60 percent of the post-show workload is a Postgres and a clean CRM away from being solved. Another 30 percent is rule-based: a lead from AAA gets this packet, a lead from CES Longevity gets that one, a private-label inquiry routes to a different person on a different day. Only the last 10 percent is actually a problem where a language model should be writing anything on your behalf.

The 60-30-10 split is the thing we care about getting right, because every time a team points an LLM at a problem that wanted a database, the spend goes up and the quality goes down. The point of the first conversation is to figure out which layer each piece of your workload belongs on. Then we build on top of your current stack, not under it.

The relevant case study

Feeld, a product company with a CTO named Andrew Santus, ran into a version of this. Too many internal workflows, not enough shared language for where AI should sit and where it should not. Eduba ran a scoped sprint: one workshop, a handful of advisory calls, a written Organizational Context Architecture, and a Strategic Operations Framework the team still references. Not a year-long engagement. Not a replatform. A short, specific sprint that put the team in a position to make its own decisions faster. That shape fits a distributor with seven SKUs and a three-vertical trade-show calendar at least as well as it fits a product company.

The relevant paper

The claim-review question (how do you keep "FDA Approved, clinically proven, dermatologist recommended" honest across seven products and four buyer types) is the kind of problem my colleague Jake's Ethics Engine work was built to address. The Ethics Engine is a psychometric assessment tool for evaluating ideological and moral patterns in LLMs. References: arxiv.org/abs/2510.11742 and github.com/RinDig/AuditEngine.
